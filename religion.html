    Buddhism

    Buddhism is a type of spirituality that helps to bring greater meaning to human life. Buddhism is a free-forming philisophy more than a religion. Followers of the philosphy seek peaceful minds and a life of moderate living.

    Some of the key virtues of Buddhism are kindness, awareness, and wisdom. Wisdom is an interdependency between intelligence, or knowledge, and emotional awareness. It is wisdom that allows us to make best choices as we interact with others in our world.

    Karma

    The spiritual reverb that is a result of the actions that avoid suffering. Acts that avoit harm and promote future benefit.

    4 Noble Truths

    1. Suffering is inherent to life.

      2. Physically: Birth, Sickness, Old Age, Death

      Mentally: Seperation from the ones we love, contact with those who we dislike, frustation of desires

    2. Recognition of the source/cause of suffering.

      3. Cravings & Desires

      Ignorance, or lack of understanding

    3. Suffering can be ended.

      4. Remove all desire, ill-will, and ignorance

      This is supreme happiness. Every step along this path leads to more joy and a happier existance. Enlightenment is a liberation; it embodies both wisdom and great compasion.

    4. There is a path to enlightenment.

      5. A moderate life is the way to happiness. Avoid the extremes of indulgence and denial.

    The Noble 8-fold Path

    1. Right Understanding
    2. Right Attitude

    5. Right Livelihood
    6. Right Effort

    An enlightened person's wisdom and compassion can aliviate the suffering of others and help them overcome thier own sufferings.

    The 5 Precepts

    The precepts are not rules to live by, but they should be followed as a guideline. The precepts colesce to: whatever speech or action one takes, it should not result in harm to others or self. A buddhist is implored to follow the precepts at all times. In the case of trespess - do not dwell on the violation; reflect and persue actions intended to remedy it.

    1. To Undertake the training to avoid taking the life of beings.
    2. To Undertake the training to avoid taking things that do not belong to you.
    3. To Undertake the training to avoid sentual misconducts.
    4. To Undertake the training to avoid false speech.
    5. To Undertake the training to avoid substances which cause intoxication or headlessness.
      6. Additional Precepts (for special days):
    6. Abstain from food at inappropriate times.
    7. Abstain from dancing, music, entertainments, use of perfumes, ornments, and other items of adornment or beautification of the person.
    8. Abstain from luxurious seat and bed.
    9. Celibacy.

    3 Jewels

    The three realms of the buddhist faith.

    1. Buddha

      2. Buddha's full name was "Siddhartha Gautama", and is also known as "Shakyamuni." He was born in Nepal over 2,500 years ago to a royal family that resided in the Himalayan foothills. Siddhartha was afforded all the luxuries of a prince and was groomed by his father to take the reins as the patriarch of the well established family. Gautama was sheltered from the unpleasant realities of life in his younger years. It wasn't until after the age of 16, Shakyamuni left the confines of his palace to be startled by what he declared as the 4 signs: an old man, a sick person, a corpse, and a monk. It was after this excursion Siddhartha renounced his lavish life to seek out a more pure, modest one; at the age of 29, despite being a declared succussor and having a wife and son, he left the palace and his family for an ascetic life.

      Just the same as Gautama's previous lavish life, he found that his pauper life too wasn't spiritually fulfiling. It wasn't until the age of 35, that Shakyamuni achieved enlightment and became what we now-a-days reffer to as the Buddha. It was through years of deep meditation that Buddha forged his understandings of a fulfiling, spiritual life that he deemed "The Middle Way."

      Buddha is not a savior; he is an icon that achieved enlightenment and shared his discoveries with others who seek spiritual fulfilment. Buddhism doesn't have a concept of a savior because it requires followers to to learn how to save themselves.
